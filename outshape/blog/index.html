Powerups

5/5/2015

One of the key gameplay tactics in Outshape is the use of powerups. In the current version of the game, there are two powerups available to use: Shields and Bombs. Both are used by placing them on a dot, thus changing what happens when an opponent uses that dot in their turn.

Shields

When you shield a dot, it becomes protected (duh). If an opponent uses one of your shielded dots in their move, they won't take that dot but they will destroy the shield. Essentially it means they have to use that dot in two moves to take it.

Bombs

Bombs have slightly higher stakes than shields. If your opponent uses one of your bomb dots in their move, it detonates and gives you the dots above, below, to the left, and to the right.

Above there's a bomb on the left, and an example of what the board would look like if an opponent hit that bomb

Temporary Shields

Okay these aren't really a "powerup" like the other two because you can't just go putting them on dots. Temporary shields show up on dots you own which are also surrounded by dots you own (or are on the edge of the board). So if there's a dot you own (or no dot at all) above, below, to the left, and to the right of a dot you own, that means it's temporarily shielded. This really affects the gameplay in Outshape. You want to try to build groupings of dots that make it hard for your opponent to take them over.

Future Powerups

I've been kicking around a couple of other ideas for powerups that I may implement in future versions of the game. If you have any thoughts, I would love to hear them.

  • Time Bombs - Sort of the opposite of the regular bomb. You lay this down and your opponent has a certain number of turns to use it in their move and thus disarm it. If they don't, it blows up and gives you the dots above, below, to the left, and to the right.
  • Shape Swap - This might either allow you to pick a different shape or swap next shapes with your opponent.

Designing Outshape

4/21/2015

The design process for Outshape has been insanely iterative. I've gone back and forth and shifted the visual style of various aspects as I've gone along.

Initial designs of the app were made with a dark background. At the time, I was really big into the darker background on Reeder.

The modal-ish views in the app were making heavy use of translucency, as it seemed like the cool thing to do in the earlier days of iOS 7. This was before iOS 8 brought decent APIs, so my solutions for implementing these designs were a bit hacky.

It's been a long road of missteps and mind changing, so I'm honestly not sure if these next mocks were just a step in the direction of the previous mocks, or a push back against translucency and gradients that led me to the current design. I think it was the latter, but I'm not positive. Regardless, as much as I liked that look, I didn't think it fit the style I was trying to hit.

I took that stark white look, and spent a lot of time figuring out how to work color into it. There was a moment when I was working on the app late one night and f.lux had really warmed the colors on my monitor. It dawned on e that I loved what it had done to the app. It looked so much nicer with warmer blues and reds and an offwhite background. So I spend some good time warming up the color palette.

One of the last design changes I've made is to give the app a little bit of depth. It was always meant to have a simple aesthetic. But I feel like I went too far and it was lacking in any sort of affordances. I've tweaked the buttons to make it clear they're buttons. And I'm still doing some iterating to find places where the design is lacking depth.

The path to Outshape's current design has been years long and full of a lot of ugly. But I think you guys are going to like the final product. And the mocks are an incomplete representation, because the app isn't complete with animations and transitions. But more on that later...

Inspiration

4/15/2015

Outshape was really born out of a love for a couple different iOS games. When the urge to make a game hit me, I was playing a lot of Dots and Letterpress. What I really started with was the desire to make sort of a two player version of Dots. I wanted to meld those two awesome games into something but I didn't really know what that something looked like.

The Inspirers

If you're not familiar with Dots, it's a fun little game where you basically draw paths to destroy colored dots.

Letterpress, on the other hand, is a two player word game. You're given a grid of letters and each player takes turns making words with those letters, and by using letters you take control of the tiles. The game ends when the whole board is controlled and whoever controls the most tiles, wins.

The Game

In the first versions of the game, it was almost impossible to describe how to play. You had to draw lines between dots and never touch the same color of dot consecutively. Two players took turns drawing lines, and the game ended when all of the dots had been claimed. This was how the game functioned for almost a year I think. Looking back it seems annoyingly complicated.

An early mind dump for the game

I can't remember what sparked the thought, but I realized the game would be way simpler if your turns were restricted to having to draw simple shapes. The game totally changed (har har) when I went down that path. I knew that was what the game would be and everything since then has been refinement and iteration.

Good artists copy, great artists steal

Outshape is a game inspired by the ideas of other awesome games. But I have this crippling fear that people will look at it and say "It's just a Dots clone". Or even worse, that the Dots developers will feel ripped off. Every time those imposter syndrome-y thoughts pop into my head, I remind myself that I've spent hundreds of hours designing, tweaking, developing, and playing this game. Like many things, it was inspired by those that came before it. But I think (or hope at least) it stands pretty well on it's own.
